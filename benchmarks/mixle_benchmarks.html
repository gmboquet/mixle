One engine, measured against the specialists.

mixle is a general, composable modeling library. These are head-to-head fit-time scaling curves against the packages built for one model each — scikit-learn and pomegranate for Gaussian mixtures, hmmlearn for HMMs. Every point is measured, and no timing is reported unless the packages land at the same log-likelihood.

The fairness contract

A speed comparison only means something if every package runs the same computation. This is enforced by the harness, not assumed — a faster fit that lands somewhere else is a different problem, not a win.

=

Same data. One array per workload, handed to each package.

=

Same initialization. Means, covariances, weights, transitions computed once and fed in. Not timed.

=

Same iteration count. Fixed EM / Baum-Welch steps, early stopping off. No one wins by taking fewer.

✓

Verified same answer. Final log-likelihoods compared every point; max gap below 1e-6.

=

Same compute budget. All pinned to 4 threads; identical BLAS.

~

Honest timing. Median of repeats after a discarded warm-up; only .fit is timed.

Hidden Markov models · Gaussian emissions

On HMMs, mixle runs 15–25× faster than hmmlearn.

Baum-Welch over many latent-regime sequences, from a shared initialization to an identical log-likelihood. mixle's forward-backward and sufficient-statistic accumulation are JIT-compiled; hmmlearn's are pure NumPy. The gap holds as data grows and widens as the model does.

Gaussian mixtures · full covariance

On GMMs, mixle matches scikit-learn exactly — and passes it at scale.

Density estimation on correlated features. All three packages reach the same optimum to eight decimals. scikit-learn and pomegranate win on small problems, where mixle's fixed per-fit overhead dominates. But mixle accumulates the covariance as a batched matmul rather than per-sample outer products, so its per-iteration cost scales better — and it overtakes near N ≈ 140k.

Accelerator · torch-CUDA engine

The same model, moved onto a GPU.

mixle's mixture fit runs unchanged on the CUDA engine — one keyword, same API, same result. A full-covariance GMM scales to half a million points on a single card (RTX 2080 Ti). This is a capability panel, not a cross-machine speedup claim.

The batched-covariance fix

N 20,000 · 8 components · dim 128, full covariance

21 GB → 2.7 GB

A full-covariance fit at dim 128 built a 21 GB per-sample, per-component tensor and OOM'd. Accumulating the covariance as a per-component gemm dropped the peak to 2.7 GB — mean log-likelihood identical to the CPU fit to 2.5e-13. That fix is what lets the column at left reach 500k points.

Honest ceiling: the per-batch sufficient statistic is still a dense (N, dim, dim) tensor, so N=1M at dim 64 (32 GB) needs chunking over N — the next fix, and the harness records it rather than hiding it.

What to take from this

Generality you don't pay for.

mixle is one composable engine, not a bag of single-model kernels. These benchmarks are the evidence that the abstraction is close to free: it matches scikit-learn's mixture to eight decimals and overtakes it past ~140k points, beats hmmlearn on HMMs by 15–25×, and runs the identical model on a GPU.

The same primitives compose into models none of these packages express at all — heterogeneous mixtures over mixed-type features, deep latent hierarchies, custom emissions — at the speeds shown here.
